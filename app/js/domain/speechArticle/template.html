The Hierarchy of Well-Formed Speech

Synglossia is based on the following theoretical model of linguistics, called the hierarchy of well-formed speech, founded on my undergraduate thesis in mathematics.

Speech

An instance of speech is any string of zero or more speech sounds. Speech sounds may also include breaks in speech, which are called pausa.

Context Language

All speech is judged to be well-formed or babble relative to the rules of a certain given contextual language, which shall here be called the context language.

1. Phonetically Well-Formed Speech

Phonetically well-formed speech is speech that contains only the speech sounds within the inventory of sounds of the context language.

Phonetic babble is speech that is not phonetically well-formed.

Babies practice phonetic babble as part of the natural language acquisition process. In later life, the ability to practice phonetic babble becomes lost as the speaker's range of sounds becomes limited to the sound inventories of the speaker's known language(s).

Phonetically well-formed speech can be partitioned into phonemes.

If the context language has an alphabetic writing system (including a deficient writing system such as an abjad), it can represent all phonetically well-formed speech.

2. Phonotactically Well-Formed Speech

Phonotactically well-formed speech is phonetically well-formed speech in which the ordering of sounds follow the phonotactic rules of the context language.

Phonotactic babble is phonetically well-formed speech that is not phonotactically well-formed.

Phonotactically well-formed speech can be partitioned into syllables. The boundaries between syllables are always at the boundaries between phonemes, but the values of phonemes in a syllable can be affected by context outside the syllable.

In languages the phonotactic rules of which allow different sets of consonant clusters at word-boundaries and at syllable-boundaries within words, phonotactic speech can also be partitioned into individual words. For this reason, it is sometimes said of certain languages such as Japanese that individual words do not exist, because the phonotactics rules of these language treat word-boundaries and syllable-boundaries within words identically.

The boundaries between words are always at the boundaries between phonemes, but the values of phonemes for a given word can be affected by contexts outside the word according to phonotactic rules.

If the context language has a moraic or syllabic writing system, it can represent all phonotactically well-formed speech.

For most adult speakers of the context language, phonotactically well-formed speech defines the boundary of speech that is pronounceable. The average adult is unable to produce phonotactic babble or phonetic babble without special training.

3. Morphologically Well-Formed Speech

Morphologically well-formed speech is phonotactically well-formed speech in which every word is a possible output of the morphology of the context language.

Morphological babble is phonotactically well-formed speech that is not morphologically well-formed.

The morphology of a language is a collection of basic morphemes and irregular constructions. Regular constructions can be formed by putting together morphemes in the morphology of the language.

Unlike words, morphemes do not always correspond to a substring of phonemes that concatenate when morphemes are combined. Sometimes morphemes are represented by transforming existing phonemes rather than adding new phonemes. An example in English is the ablaut of the vowel in the word "foot" as a marker of the plural number.

In morphologically well-formed speech, every word has a part of speech and assigned values according to grammatical categories, but semantic meaning is not considered.

In morphologically well-formed speech, the individual words make sense, but the way in which the words are put together may not make sense grammatically or semantically.

If the context language has a logographic writing system, it can represent all morphologically well-formed speech.

Most nonsense speech coined by adults is morphological babble. Examples include nonsense syllables in music and character catch-phrases in comic books and television shows. Words of onomatopoeic etymology originate from morphological babble.

4. Syntactically Well-Formed Speech

Syntactically well-formed speech is morphologically well-formed speech in which words are in proper order according to the rules of syntax of the context language.

Syntactic babble is morphologically well-formed speech that is not syntactically well-formed.

Syntactically well-formed speech can be partitioned into sentences. The boundaries between sentences are always at the boundaries between words.

In syntactically well-formed speech, the individual words make sense, and the way they are put together grammatically makes sense, but the way that they are used semantically in the sentence might not make sense.

5. Topically Well-Formed Speech

Topically well-formed speech is syntactically well-formed speech in which all words and usages of words conform to concepts that already exist in a given topic of conversation.

Topical babble is syntactically well-formed speech that is not topically well-formed.

The sentence "Colorless green ideas sleep furiously" is an example provided by Noam Chomsky of syntactically well-formed English speech that is not topically well-formed, because as of most usual contexts, some adjectives such as "colorless" and "green" have contradictory meanings. In order for this sentence to be topically well-formed, it must follow after a context that introduces the words in such a way as to validate their usage in this sentence.

In topically well-formed speech, the individual words make sense, the way words are put together grammatically make sense, and the way words are used semantically in the sentence make sense.

Summary

Altogether, the various levels of well-formed speech produce the following hierarchy.

  1. Topically well-formed speech.
  2. Syntactically well-formed speech.
  3. Morphologically well-formed speech.
  4. Phonotactically well-formed speech.
  5. Phonetically well-formed speech.
  6. Speech.

Altogether, this means that a language must have the following properties in order to be able to define the highest level of well-ordered speech.

  • An inventory of phonemes.
  • A set of rules describing the proper order of phonemes in speech.
  • An inventory of morphemes that can be used to form words, and irregular words.
  • A set of rules describing the proper order of words in speech.
  • An inventory of concepts, and a set of logical rules describing how these concepts must interact with each other.

The concept of a language according to this model is stricter than the usual concept of a language in the scientific sense. English, French, Chinese, and so forth are examples of languages that defined well-formed speech up through the syntactic level, but excluding the topical level. For this reason, topical babble is considered proper use of language in the scientific sense, despite that it has no apparent usage in a real-world context that would not identity it as babble. Examples of languages in the sense of this model may be something like "sales English," or "medical English."

Subdividing a language in the usual scientific sense in this way is not unique to the topical level. In fact, distinct dialects within a language, such as General American English and Southern American English, differ on levels as low as the phonetic level.
